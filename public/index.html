St. Pauli School of Test-driven Development

Principles

  1. Start with a simple test at the API-level of your Unit under Test (UuT).
  2. Without using mocks, work from the outside to the inside of your UuT. Reduce the scope of a UuT by using stubs that pretend to have solved one or more subtasks of the UuT.
  3. Grow tests and implementation slow and steady by writing the obvious implementation, fake it 'till you make it and triangulate.
  4. Distinguish impure functions into non-determinism and side-effects. Test non-deterministic functions with stubs or fakes and model side-effects as data structures whose correctness can be tested easily. Write a very thin layer which interprets the side-effect data structures and emits the side effects. Test this layer with integration tests and acceptance tests.
  5. During development, treat your suit of tests as append-only. Do not delete or comment out a test that is formally correct, but conflicts with your current development state. Instead, revert to a state where you can make continuous progress again.
  6. If there are stubs in the UuT, replace the stubs with their real implementation by making them the new UuT and go to step 1.
  7. Always add a validation test after each UuT has been finished to avoid overfitting to the training set that you used to develop the UuT.

FAQ

Why another school? Don't we have enough already?

The St. Pauli school of TDD started as a tongue-in-cheek response to the new founded Munich school of TDD by fellow software crafter David Völkel, but developed in a useful TDD style of its own.

How does the St. Pauli school of TDD differ from other schools?

All schools formulate an opinion on three common aspects:

School St. Pauli Detroit/Chicago London Munich
Direction Outside-In Inside-Out Outside-In Outside-In
First Test Case Simple Simple Simple Complex
Use of Mocks avoid avoid embrace avoid

As shown above, the St. Pauli school of TDD differs in 1 out of 3 aspects from every other school.

Besides these aspects, the St. Pauli school has four requirements, which are not integral part of the other schools:

  • During development, treat your suit of tests as append only
  • Always add a validation test after each UuT has been finished
  • Prefer pure units
  • Test non-determinism and side-effects differently

Isn't TDD a waste of time, since there are so much better development techniques out there?

Even though Thinking before programming, Hammock-driven Development, Property-Based Testing, REPL-Driven development and Type-driven Development are useful techniques, this does neither imply that TDD is useless, nor that the aforementioned techniques and Test-driven Development are mutually exclusive.
